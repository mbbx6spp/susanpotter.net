How to make seemingly difficult decisions

Date: October 17, 2020 Last modified: October 17, 2020

Originally published as a Twitter thread starting here.

Anyone who has spent any time in either a technical or people management leadership position in software development knows, you need to make seemingly difficult decisions all the time.

This week I coached a coworker through making what seemed like a difficult decision by coaching him through the following process.

Process outline

  1. Identify your options.

  2. List the risks & possible mitigations (for each option).

  3. Relatively rank the risk ranges (from mitigated to unmitigated, for each option).

  4. List benefits (for each option).

  5. Decide and take next steps!

Note that steps 1 and 5 act on all the options. Steps 2 through 4 inclusive are per identified option (from step 1).

Step 1: Identifying options

In this step, you really want to think creatively. There have been many times I previously didnt fully explore the option space which limited my ability to get more of what I wanted.

Realistically, you want to limit the number of options to about 3-5. So pick the best 3, 4 or 5 options as you are brainstorming.

Two options is perfectly fine but don't trick yourself into believing this is the full option space if it still feels like an impossible decision at step 5. In that case, revisit this step and start over from here.

Step 2: List the risks & possible mitigations for each option

List our what risks each option has then try to think how could you limit the risks in each case. How likely is it that you can mitigate the risks in each case? How much control do you really have?

Step 3: Relatively rank the risk range (from mitigated to unmitigated)

It isn't a quantitative step nor is it objective. In fact, you want this to be somewhat subjective because you are choosing which problems and the possible problems and range of magnitude you'd rather have.

Step 4: List benefits for each option

Remind yourself what the benefits are. Filter out options that have marginal or unimportant benefits to you.

If you have exactly one option left then the next step should be easy. :)

Step 5: Decision and take next steps!

Give yourself a reasonable timebox to do this step given the options left after step 4. Sometimes you will not have full control of how long you can take to decide (e.g. job offer deadline) but try to negotiate if needed to avoid opportunities closing on you.

General notes

This is not for every kind of decision. Some decisions should be obvious to make given your own values. This is a process generally needed when upon first look at the problem, all possible solutions look bad.

It doesn't work for the times you really need to make a decision in an instant, however, you might want ask the car salesperson again if that offer might still stand. You might be surprised.

Your state of mind during this process matters. Try to get your mind to be calm. Try the methods you know work for you to eliminate anxiety and stress about making this decision. Try to remember you might have more control of the situation than you initially thought you did.

If you are faced with being in the path of a stampeed of woolly mammoths while hunting for your next meal, this is not the process you need. Run for your life out of their path!

One last note about this process is that you don't want to take a long time on the final step. If that step takes a long time, then maybe you haven't explored the option space well in step 1. It might save you time and pain if you revisit step 1 and rethink the possibilities available to you.

Hope this helps.
