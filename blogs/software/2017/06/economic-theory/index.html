Principles of economics applied to software development, reliability engineering, and technical leadership

One of my favorite subjects as a lay person is economics from which I have found many useful applications of core principles relating to my work in software development, technical leadership as well as my recent reliability engineering roles.

This is a post to share some of my thoughts around this.

A few ideas from economics

The ideas from economics that have resonated with me at work are:

opportunity cost

Looking at what you lose by choosing a specific activity over others.

causal inference with natural experiments

This uses results from a natural experiment to answer questions which have enormous potential for distributed systems (I think).

comparative advantage

This is not an intuitive idea and typically used in international trade policy to determine which trading partner would be better for the bigger dynamic system to be producing even when they aren't the best at it globally. This has implications for my work in the best people to assign work to given other constraints.

thinking on the margins

This inspects when marginal costs outweigh marginal benefit. This has implications for me in my work when scoping, and specifying infrastructure and system architectures.

lump of labor fallacy

A fallacy that is a common misconception in society at large where people think there is a fixed amount of work so automation or immigration yields job losses for manual native workers. There are more deeper insights here too. Implications in operations, "DevOps" culture, etc.

Opportunity Cost

Opportunity cost is the idea that to engage in some activity we are typically making ourselves or others unavailable to do other activities for that dedicated duration.

Paired with the idea of core competencies, which is our distilled understanding of what makes our company, product, and/or team special, we can pick more strategically which parts of our application or platform our in-house product team builds versus buys.

Causal inference with natural experiments

To determine causality scientists of all stripes typically design experiments, collect observations, and then analyze those results derived from the observations. Today most designed experiments are randomized.

However, when it is too expensive to conduct a designed randomized experiment or it's difficult to replicate the conditions of the real world another category of experiment might be used. These are known as natural experiments where recorded observations from the real world environment can be analyzed in a meaningful way.

Consider in the 90s when New Jersey increased their minimum wage from $4.25 to $5.05 per hour. Adjacent Pennsylvania retained its minimum wage at $4.25 (the price before the increase in New Jersey) so economists surveyed a large number of fast food restaurants across both states to try to detect whether raising the minimum wage would negatively impact employment growth this in industry. The paper can explain its conclusions better than I can, but the relevant part for our discussion is that state legislation changed outside of the economists' control; the economists didn't setup the conditions of either New Jersey or Pennsylvania so they could run the experiment, they merely exploited naturally occurring conditions to extract observations that are meaningful to an interesting question of causality, e.g. in this case, does raising the minimum wage lead to lower employment growth.

As a former software reliability engineer (SRE) I find myself wondering whether and/or how a change in one part of the system will impact the behavior of the rest, much like an economist studying factors that might impact employment growth.
